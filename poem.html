The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock

Ragged Claws

By Helen Du

Silent woods
Lonely trees
No one around
No one next to me

Little boy
Cool summer breeze
When he saw me
He screamed

Discontent
Anger
Deep envy

There and then
My rough hands
Found their place
Around his neck

The earth accepted him
The wind tucked him in
The world had one less boy
To hate a monster like me

I would never be
Any less monstrous than he

Little Frankenstein
Why do you upset me?
Could you have not
Held in that last scream?

Innocent angel
Your glorious features
Defy my existence
If you are diamond
Then I am coal

Wherever I go
They make me to be less
Of a human than they are

Should I be the only
Deprived of attributes that
Make you beautiful
And lack thereof
Make me beastly?

Rationale:

The poem “Ragged Claws” tells of the murder of William Frankenstein from the monster’s perspective. It is meant to represent
the lack of self worth that both J. Alfred Prufrock and the monster have. The word “coal” in the line “Then I am coal” highlights
a lack of self worth, as coal is a resource that is less valued than “diamond” (from the previous line). Both section 7 and the
poem describe the individuals as “ragged claws” and “coal” (respectively)—nothing of particular significance or aesthetic beauty.
As Prufrock continues to ponder his insignificance and what will become of his existence, so too does the monster reflect on his
place in Frankenstein’s world.

Afraid

By Carrie Men

What is it I seek on this path alone,
When I cannot feel this body I call my own?
From a distance, I spot a tall stranger
Suddenly, I feel a sense of danger

Straight up ahead there is such darkness,
As if there was no light to harness.
Yet towards the darkness I am drawn,
And I see I am nothing but a pawn.

The stranger! I remember as I look around,
But he is nowhere to be found.
My eyes wander for anything I can find,
And then I feel a shiver up my spine.

I look ahead, and again there he is,
Staring at me with hollow eyes of his.
Through the darkness, I find him in the centre,
Beckoning me, with his hand, to enter.

Rationale:

The poem “Afraid” represents the fear of death of the speaker from “The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock”, who claims to have
seen the “eternal Footman”, the Grim Reaper. Aside from the fear of death, this poem also portrays the fear of the unknown and
the unwillingness to take risks. The speaker appears lost after death, wandering with no goal in mind, and eventually finding
a path to the darkness and the unknown. The tall stranger spotted by the speaker is the representation of the “eternal Footman”
who has come to take the unwilling speaker to the afterlife, as daunting as death may be.
