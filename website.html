The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock

Platter

Line: Though I have seen my head (grown slightly bald) brought in upon a platter, I am no prophet—and here's no great matter

John the Baptist Beheaded

Rationale:

This line of the poem is an allusion to a Bible verse, describing the beheading of John the Baptist, who was said to be a prophet. In
summary, John misspoke against the new wife of King Herod, Herodias, by telling King Herod that it is improper to take in his brother’s
wife. This lead Herodias to hold a grudge against John and she wanted to kill him. An opportunity came when King Herod held a banquet on
his birthday, in which his step-daughter Salome danced for him. He promised her she could have anything she wanted, up to half his kingdom,
and Salome asked for John the Baptist’s head on a platter, of which she presented to her mother Herodias. The speaker of the poem claims
to also have seen his head on a platter, but because he is not a prophet, he believes his situation is less important and of “no great
matter”, emphasizing his sense of inferiority.

Flicker

Line: I have seen the moment of my greatness flicker

Darkness and devils!
Saddle my horses; call my train together:
Degenerate bastard! I'll not trouble thee.
Yet have I left a daughter.
- Act I, Scene iv, King Lear, King Lear

Rationale:

This short excerpt from Shakespeare’s King Lear, Act I Scene iv, is spoken by King Lear himself right after he was denied by his daughter
Goneril to stay at her residence. Although it is not said how, the speaker says he has seen “the moment of his greatness flicker”.
Paralleling that, Lear’s authority as a king appears to be stripped away by a daughter who he gave half his land and power to, and his
authority dims. In a fit of rage, Lear storms out of Goneril’s residence, reminding himself that he has another daughter. This gives him
the sense that his authority, as a father and as a king, has returned. As a result, this short passage demonstrates how Lear’s “greatness”,
his power and authority, is shown to “flicker”, just as how the speaker’s moment of greatness was said to have flickered.
